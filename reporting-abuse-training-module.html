Lesson 1 of 5

Your duty — and your protection

Objective: understand that reporting is part of your job, not an optional act of courage — and that you are protected when you do it.

You are the advocate

Many of our Service Users see one person regularly: you. You are often the only trusted adult who crosses their doorway. That means you are their eyes, their ears, and — when something is wrong — their voice.

Reporting a concern isn't "getting involved" or "causing trouble." When you accepted a role at Liberty Living, you accepted a duty to report. It is part of the job, exactly like clocking your visit or following the care plan. A concern you keep to yourself is a Service User left unprotected.

In plain English You are never expected to be certain. You are never expected to prove anything. You are only expected to pass on what you saw or heard, the same day. Deciding what it means is someone else's job.

"But what if I'm wrong?" — you are protected

This is the fear that stops people reporting, so let's deal with it head-on:

  • Your identity is protected. It's completely understandable to worry "what if the client's next of kin finds out it was me?" — but that fear shouldn't stop you reporting. What you tell us is handled on a strict need-to-know basis, and your name is not shared with the people your concern is about. (In some cases — for example a Garda investigation — the law may require limited information to be shared. If that ever applied to you, you would be told.)
  • It will not fall back on you. If you report honestly, with reasonable grounds for your concern, you are protected from any penalisation at work — even if the concern turns out to be unfounded. This protection is set out in Irish law (Protected Disclosures Act 2014, as amended in 2022) and in Liberty Living policy (QA-37).
  • You won't be dragged into it. Once you've reported, trained safeguarding staff take it from there. You don't investigate, you don't confront anyone, you don't have to face the person involved.
  • Reporting about a colleague or manager is protected too. If the concern involves someone inside Liberty Living — at any level — the same protections apply.

Lesson 2 of 5

Recognising abuse

Objective: identify the seven categories of abuse and the real-world signs you might meet on a home visit.

Abuse is rarely obvious. It hides in small things: an empty fridge, a changed mood, a relative who never leaves the room. There are seven categories you must know. Tap each card to see what it looks like in a real home.

Tap each card to open it ↓

Physical +

Deliberate harm to the body. Hitting, slapping, rough handling, misuse of medication or restraint.

Signs: unexplained bruises (especially in unusual places — inner arms, torso), marks in the shape of fingers or objects, injuries that don't match the explanation, flinching when approached.

Psychological +

Harm to a person's mind and dignity. Shouting, threats, humiliation, blaming, controlling, isolating them from people they love.

Signs: a Service User who becomes withdrawn, anxious or tearful; who seems afraid of a particular person; whose personality seems to change when someone enters the room.

Financial +

Theft or misuse of money, property or benefits. Often by someone trusted. This is the most under-detected form of abuse.

Signs: pension or cash going missing, unpaid bills despite adequate income, sudden changes to wills or bank arrangements, a relative controlling all money while the Service User goes without essentials.

Sexual +

Any sexual act the person has not or cannot consent to.

Signs: unexplained injuries or soreness in intimate areas, torn or stained underclothing, sudden fear of personal care, sexualised behaviour or language that is new, hints or partial disclosures ("I don't like it when he visits at night").

Neglect +

Failing to meet basic needs — by a carer, a family member, or a service.

Signs: no food in the fridge, medication not given, cold house, pressure sores, soiled bedding or clothing, weight loss, missed medical appointments, being left alone for long periods.

Discriminatory +

Abuse driven by who the person is — their age, race, religion, gender, sexuality or disability.

Signs: slurs or "jokes" targeting identity, poorer treatment compared to others, services or care withheld because of who they are.

Institutional +

When an organisation's routines are put above people's needs — including ours.

Signs: rigid rules that ignore individual needs, staff speaking harshly or roughly to Service Users, corners cut on visits, a culture where concerns get brushed off. Yes — this includes reporting colleagues. You are protected when you do.
Watch the people around them too Abuse often comes from someone close — a family member, a next of kin, a regular visitor. If a relative's behaviour towards your Service User feels inappropriate, controlling or just strange, that feeling is reportable. You don't need a category. You don't need proof. "Something isn't right here" is enough to pick up the phone.

Lesson 3 of 5

When someone tells you

Objective: respond safely and correctly when a Service User discloses abuse or harm (SOP-005).

Imagine this

"You're helping Margaret with her breakfast when she suddenly grips your hand and says, quietly: 'My son takes my pension every week. I've nothing left for the heating. Please don't tell anyone — he'd be so angry.'"

What you do in the next sixty seconds matters enormously. Get it right and Margaret is protected. Get it wrong and she may never tell anyone again.

Always do

  • Stay calm. Listen. Let them speak at their own pace.
  • Use open questions only — "Can you tell me more?"
  • Take it seriously and say so: "Thank you for telling me."
  • Tell them gently that you must share this to keep them safe.
  • End the visit safely and normally.
  • Write down what they said word for word, as soon as possible.
  • Report it the same day.

Never do

  • Promise to keep it secret — you can't, and you mustn't.
  • Show shock, disgust or disbelief.
  • Ask leading questions — "Did he hit you?"
  • Confront the person they've named.
  • Start investigating or gathering evidence yourself.
  • Discuss it with colleagues, family or anyone else not in the reporting chain.

Why you can never promise secrecy

It feels kind in the moment — but a promise of secrecy is a promise to leave them in danger. Instead, say something like:

Words that work "Thank you for trusting me with this. I can't keep it just between us, because I want to keep you safe — but I'll only share it with the people whose job it is to help. You've done nothing wrong."

Why word-for-word matters

Your written record may later be read by safeguarding professionals — possibly even Gardaí. What the person actually said, in their own words, is powerful evidence. Your interpretation is not. Write facts, not opinions: what you saw, what you heard, when, and who was present. Nothing more.

Lesson 4 of 5

How to report — and what happens next

Objective: follow the reporting pathway the same day, every time — and understand where your responsibility ends.

The reporting pathway

1
You notice, or you're toldA sign, a disclosure, or just a feeling that something isn't right.
2
Make safe, note the factsEnd the visit safely. Write down what you saw or heard — word for word, facts only.
3
Call the office immediatelyPhone your line manager or the office the same day — every time, no exceptions. For anything serious, call; don't rely on email or text.
4
Complete the Incident ReportYour line manager will guide you through it, and may ask for a short follow-up email too. Your word-for-word notes go in here — it's the written record of what you reported.
5
The DSO takes overThe DSO carries out a preliminary screening the same day and, where needed, notifies the HSE Safeguarding & Protection Team or the Gardaí. Serious cases go to the CEO the same day.

📋 What the Incident Report actually looks like

This is the real form your line manager will guide you through (Incident Report Form 2.0). Getting familiar with it now means you'll know exactly what to have ready — get the details noted at the time so this is quick to fill in:

  • Client Name
  • HCA Name
  • Address of Incident
  • Date of Incident
  • Time of Incident
  • Other Persons Involved in Incident
  • Person Making Report
  • Reported To
  • Full Description of the Incident — including witnesses, what happened, injuries (if any), and where it occurred

Open the Incident Report form ↗

This link is for familiarisation now — you'll be guided through completing it for real by your line manager after you report a concern.

Emergencies are different If a Service User is in immediate danger, call 999 first. Then report through the pathway as soon as it's safe to do so.
Your notes are personal data — handle them with care The word-for-word record you make contains sensitive personal information, so data protection law (GDPR) applies. Keep it simple: write facts only, keep your notes secure and private until handed over, transfer them into the Incident Report, and never keep copies — and never photograph a Service User, their home or their documents on a personal phone. Sharing safeguarding information with the DSO and statutory agencies is lawful and required; sharing it with anyone else is a data breach.

Where your job ends

Once you've reported and completed the form, your part is done — and done well. You do not investigate. You do not question the Service User further. You do not confront anyone. You do not follow up with the family. Trained professionals — the DSO, the HSE, the Gardaí — take it from there. Attempting to investigate yourself can destroy evidence and put the Service User at greater risk.

Lesson 5 of 5

Open Disclosure & Protected Disclosure — in plain English

Objective: explain what these two terms mean and why neither of them should ever make you afraid to speak up.

Two similar-sounding phrases, two completely different ideas. Both exist to build the same thing: a culture where nothing gets hidden.

🔓 Open Disclosure — being honest when something goes wrong

What it means: when something goes wrong in a person's care — a mistake, an incident, a near miss — Liberty Living tells the Service User (and their family, where appropriate) openly, honestly and promptly, through the proper management channel. Leadership explains what happened, apologises, and explains what's being done to stop it happening again.

What it means for you: report it to your line manager immediately — never cover up, minimise or "keep the head down" about something that went wrong, yours or anyone else's. But the disclosure conversation with the Service User or their family is not your job: you never explain, apologise for, or discuss the incident with the client yourself, at any stage. Report to your line manager and let them handle it from there. Honesty with your line manager after a mistake is not the thing that gets people in trouble at Liberty Living. Hiding one is. And it's worth knowing: under Irish law, an apology given as part of open disclosure is not an admission of fault or liability — the law was written this way precisely so honesty is safe.

Policy: QA-13, Incident Management and Open Disclosure · consistent with the HSE Open Disclosure framework and the Patient Safety (Notifiable Incidents and Open Disclosure) Act 2023.

🛡 Protected Disclosure — the law that protects you when you speak up

What it means: if you report a concern about wrongdoing — abuse, neglect, unsafe practice, misconduct — and you have reasonable grounds to believe it, Irish law (the Protected Disclosures Act 2014, as amended in 2022) protects you from penalisation at work. You cannot be dismissed, sidelined, demoted, given worse shifts or treated unfairly for speaking up. This applies even if the concern involves a colleague, a manager, or the organisation itself.

What it means for you:

  • Your identity is protected. You can report a concern without your name being shared with the person you're reporting about — including a Service User's next of kin. Being worried "what if they find out it was me" is completely understandable, but it's not a reason to stay quiet: your identity is kept confidential on a need-to-know basis. (Limited legal exceptions exist — for example where the Gardaí require it — and you would be informed.)
  • Once reported, you don't need to be involved in the investigation.
  • A concern raised honestly that turns out to be unfounded is still protected. The only thing that isn't protected is a report you know to be false — honesty is the whole test.

Policy: QA-37, Protected Disclosures · Protected Disclosures Act 2014 (as amended 2022).

The one-line version Open Disclosure = you report honestly to your line manager when something goes wrong — never to the Service User yourself.
Protected Disclosure = you are safe when you tell us something is wrong.
